Analysis of the Predator-Prey System

Rabbits and Foxes

We will now apply our machinery to the predator-prey system. Consider the rabbits and foxes example from a few days ago, with simple choices of parameters shown below. \[ \begin{align*} \frac{df}{dt} &= -3f + rf\\ \frac{dr}{dt} &= 4r - 2 rf \end{align*} \] In your homework you just found the nullclines, which hopefully looked like this:

In this picture, the \(x\)-nullclines are for rabbits, and the \(y\)-nullclines are for foxes.

Now we ask the computer to make a direction field on top of the nullclines. It will look like this.

Lets practice reading information from this picture.

  1. The most striking feature is that the arrows form large swirls. This means we expect solutions to be periodic, meaning our populations will oscillate.
  2. There are two equilibria: one at \((0,0)\) and one at \((3,2)\). (We can pretend that we measure animals in hundreds, so this might mean 300 rabbits and 200 foxes.)
  3. Along an \(x\)-nullclines, the vectors only point up/down. Also, along \(y\)-nullclines, the vectors only point left/right.

Lets look at a trajectory starting from \((6,5)\).

Indeed, as expected, the trajectory closes back on itself. In words, heres what happens. Again, we imagine a movie of the dot at the location \((6,5)\), where time starts. In this order, heres what the populations do:

  1. The number of foxes grows slightly, all the while rabbits are decreasing dramatically due to the large number of foxes eating them.
  2. The rabbits reach a minimum population, and a small time later, in response, the foxes numbers dwindle very quickly, going from a max of 600 foxes down to what looks to be near 0 foxes (maybe a small number, like 20).
  3. Once the fox population is almost gone, the rabbits can flourish: this happens on the bottom of that egg shape. The fox population is almost constant (which shows up since the bottom is nearly flat) but the rabbits population takes off (which shows up in the fact that the trajectory shoots off to the right).
  4. Once the rabbits population soars too high, there is plenty of food for the foxes to start reproducing, and once they start to do so, the rabbits begin declining in numbers again.

The fact that the trajectory cycles back on itself (we call this closing up) means that in this model, this behaviour cycles on forever.

By looking at these (computer) made graphs, we should notice some aspects:

  1. The solutions are periodic: they repeat every so often. (They are not trig functions, however!)
  2. The prey populations spike before the predators do.

The differential equations actually told us the correct locations for the peaks and troughs of our populations.
